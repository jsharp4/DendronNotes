Summary

Turing's Cathedral is not a book about Alan Turing. While set in his era, and even intersects with rough location when he visited the United States and met with Claude Shannon to discuss the theory of computing, the book is about his contemporaries at the Institue for Advanced Study and the computers which were built to instantiate the universal computing machine.

Noteworthy Ideas

  • The Institue for Advanced Study was not originally planned to be at Princeton. Princeton leadership lobbied hard for locations nearby the campus, and continued to spend large sums on expansion nearby. #qq Who was the leader of Princeton at this time, and who became the leader of IAS?

  • The Institue for Advanced Study benefitted greatly from German hostility towards Jews near the onset of WWII. Hungarian John Von Neumann, German Albert Einstein, Austrian Kurt Godel, and Polish Stan Ulam all fled Europe to make significant contributions to computing in the United States. The Institue for Advanced Study was willing to jump through hoops, lie about teaching positions (in Godel's case), and invest significant funds to facilitate immigration.

  • John Von Neumann was likely the most significant figure to computer engineering in history. He contributed heavily to the designs of the ENIAC, EDVAC, MANIAC, along with applications in meteorology, the atomic bomb, and the Monte Carlo method. He consulted for the U.S. military, at Los Alamos, IBM, RCA, and served as department head at the Institue for Advanced Study.

  • John Von Neumann was a genius, known for making jumbled ideas of other clear. Also quirky, he frequently bought and totalled Cadillacs, and lost his lisence for speeding (quote: "Because they wouldn't sell me a tank"). He had great interest in game theory (which he more or less invented) and gambling.
